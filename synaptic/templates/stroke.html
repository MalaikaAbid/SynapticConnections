What is Stroke?

For our brain cells to function properly, they require a constant supply of oxygen and nutrients that are delivered to the brain through our blood. When blood flow to the brain becomes compromised by a blockage in a blood vessel that supplies the brain (ischemic) or by the bursting of a blood vessel in the brain (hemorrhagic), brain cells that do not receive enough nutrients begin to die, resulting in a stroke. In most cases, the stroke will only affect a portion of the brain, which is why symptoms tend to differ from patient to patient. Because of the way the brain is wired, strokes that occur in the right side of the brain commonly affect function of the left side of the body, and vice versa.

Post-Stroke Support and Rehabilitation

While the symptoms of a stroke can be severe, many of them are common and improve over time and with rehabilitation. Your recovery team may consist of a variety of doctors, nurses, therapists and dieticians.

  • Communication:

    Common therapy techniques involve matching words to pictures, naming objects in pictures, practicing reading comprehension and exercises to strengthen the muscles needed to speak.

  • Use of Arms and Legs:

    Rehabilitation may work on strengthening your muscles, refining movements and the proper positioning of joints, through the use of splints or orthotics, physiotherapy, exercise, and/or BotoxÂ® injections.

  • Bowel and Bladder:

    Depending on the cause of your symptoms, physiotherapy, dietary changes and/or lifestyle changes may be recommended.

  • Swallowing:

    A team of speech pathologists, dieticians, and occupational therapists may provide exercises and tips for safe eating.
